The Amazon Rainforest!

The Amazon Rainforest is the largest tropical rainforest in the world, as well as one of the most biodiverse places on the planet. The forest plays a critical role in regulating the planets climate and supports millions of species.

Locations

The Amazon spans across nine South American countries, 60% of the forest is within Brazil however it also extends to:

  • Peru
  • Columbia
  • Venezuela
  • Ecuador
  • Bolivia
  • Guyana
  • Suriname
  • France (via French Guiana)

Why It's Important

  1. Climate Regulation: The Amazon acts as lungs for the Earth, absorbing large amounts of carbon dioxide and producing oxygen. This helps to stabilize global temperatures and weather patterns.
  2. Biodiversity: The Amazon is home to over 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, more than 400 mammal species, and millions of insects.
  3. Indigenous Communities: Over 400 Indigenous groups live in the Amazon, many maintain traditional lifestyles and deep ecological knowledge of the forest.

Amazon under threat

According to WWF, "transportation and energy infrastructure are essential for national and regional development, but when they are poorly planned, negative impacts can exceed short-term benefits. For example, building new roads exposes previously inaccessible areas of forest to illegal and unsustainable logging, as well as illegal or unplanned settlements and agricultural expansion."

Along with the negative impacts of poorly planned infrastructure, deforestation is harmfully affecting the rainforests sutainability. Green Peace scientists warn that "if even only 5–8% more of the Amazon is lost to deforestation, it could reach a tipping point, meaning the rainforest will not be able to produce enough rain to sustain itself. This would have a disastrous impact on the whole world’s climate."
